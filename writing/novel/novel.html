“I never”

Clang.

“counted,”

Clang.

“not even among my”

Clang. Pant.

“remotest hopes,”

Clang. clangclang.

“that I would die”

Pant. Shuffle back. Guard up.

“by the sword of a foe”

Lunge. Clang. Parry. Back.

“as illustrious as”

Clang.

“the bastard boy duke,” said the man who would, within the hour, kill Eathel. Eathel was indeed a bastard, but the Duke was his father. Men tacked it on to ridicule him the title he might have had.

Clang. Swing. Dodge. Clang back. Both shuffle away.

They circled one another en guard. The swords that had been making the racketous grima in the little holy clearing, deep in the woods, held up before them, their ends occasionally touching in a few nervous taps.

“My god. You look so much like your father,” said the man.

This was interesting. So this attacking stranger not only recognized him, but apparently knew his father well enough to see the strong resemblance between the Duke and his off-born boy everyone invariably remarked on.

A rapid clanging of sword on sword. The woods about them muffled the sharp report of whooshing swinging metal instantly stopped against metal. But they had enough space to be loud here, encircled as they were with the rune-carved rocks of the old sacred site that stood as silent sentinels of their duel, holding back the forest a bit.

“You are so certain of defeat,” said Eathel. “I have not beaten you yet.”

Their blades clanged again, binding for a moment as both men panted in brief respite.

“Oh, but you will,” said Eathel’s foil.

The man feinted a lunge. Eathel setzte him ab. The man shuffled back into a resting guard, his sword up and at attention in front of him.

“I have heard tales of your exploits abroad,” Eathel’s opponent said.

Clang. Shuffle. Clang.

“And of your facility,”

Clang. Tapping, testing his left again.

“With the blade,” said the man, lunging, but overreaching, stumbling a bit.

Eathel parried, but it was more a tap on the blade and more of a dodge.

Don’t engage in his conversation, said Sera, the woman who lived in his head. You’re on an investigation; this is your chief lead. He clearly wants to talk. Let him.

He nodded at her, though she had no face or head to nod at, nothing at all really to see to her, save a slim white, glowing sliver of a mouth, where a woman’s mouth would be if a real one stood by him.

“They say you are guided by angels,” the man said, smiling. They were both stepping around one another in a circle.

Can you hear anything in his accent, Eathel asked Sera. I was listening for the same thing. Could be anywhere in Tera or beyond. He talks like someone who hasn’t stayed in one place very long.

“Well, then that makes it sound like I have a lot of help,” said Eathel. “Some skill I have if heaven must send me aid.”

The man laughed. “where I am from, our god is angry. It is a warrior god. When its acolytes displease it, it makes war on them.”

“Sounds like an unfair fight,” said Eathel.

“And it would be, for our god is strong,” said the man. “But it does not itself strap on its war regalia”

“to contend with mere mortals.

No, for those nations that have displeased it, god sends its men angels to spread evil feeling among the offending nation’s neighbors.

But to brave warriors like you, it sends its women angels who rally about the warriors who”

He’s edging closer, said Sera. I see it, said Eathel. This yarn is intended to distract you from some kind of attack, she said. I know, he said. Well, I have a vested interest in your heart continuing to beat; you ought not be surprised I take an interest in your physical wellbeing.

The left muscles tense, said Sera. It’s now.

A smooth, elegant, deadly move and the man was suddenly on Eathel’s outside right with his attacker’s sword somehow transposed to the man’s outside hand. If Eathel hadn’t been looking for the move and ready for it, the man would have had nothing in his path to Eathel’s armpit and up.

But instead, Eathel swung back and out to the right, staying aside the man’s thrust. His sword was too long to get a good thrust of his own into the man, but he could punch the crossbar of his sword into the man’s face. This, Eathel did, with the degree of strength and ferocity he only ever seemed to be able to harness when he was about to die.

The blow landed with a thick, wet slap right where Eathel wanted it to. right in his attacker’s cheekbone

Eathel felt elation and power. His hate, borne of fear, for this man flash froze into a cool and insidious hate for this man, this one borne of anger. Kill him? How dare he try to take his life from him.

The man yelped and screamed loudly enough Eathel may have knocked out a few teeth through his cheek; he stumbling back, momentarily drunk on a strong whiskey of pain. But he quickly got control of himself. Still, Eathel could tell the wound was hurting and starting to take up more attention.
